## 2. Format: demo-led, with a thin slide spine

You asked: slides, demo, or both? **Both — but the demo is the star.**

- A **live demo** is your strongest weapon. Saaksh is visual and instant. Watching
  it turn a blank 108-field format into a cited, colour-coded gap plan in under a
  minute is the moment that sells it. Consultants feel the time-saving viscerally.
- A **pure demo** rambles and can break live. So wrap it in **~7 slides** that
  carry the story and act as a safety net.

**Two non-negotiable safety rules:**
1. **Pre-load a sample client before the call.** Don't type into the form live in
   front of people — fill it beforehand (e.g. a steel manufacturer with a couple of
   filings), so you can jump straight to the report. Have the form filled in a
   second browser tab too.
2. **Have a backup.** Take screenshots of every screen you'll show (or a 60-second
   screen recording). If the live site hiccups on the call, you switch to images
   and never lose the room.

### The hard rule: don't pin a price
Pro isn't billed yet, and the pricing model isn't decided. If you name a number on the
call you'll either anchor too low or scare someone off — and you can't change it
gracefully later. So present Pro as **early access**: "the Pro tools are in early
access; if you want them for a live client, talk to me and I'll set you up." Then treat
any pricing question as **research** — the room is full of your exact buyers, so let
them tell you what they'd pay. Cheapest pricing study you'll ever run.

### What Pro actually is (the reframe — say this, not "the BRSR collect feature")
Don't sell Pro as one feature. Sell it as **the consultant's compliance workspace**:
- **The data-collection engine** — and it isn't BRSR-only. Strip the label and it's
  "chase numbers from a client's team, track, remind, attribute, draft." That same
  engine works for GRI, CDP, GHG inventories, CBAM, supplier assessments. BRSR is just
  the first template.
- **A growing module library** — emissions today; net-zero, CBAM, waste next.
- **The knowledge centers** — "how to actually answer Principle 5", used year-round.

This pre-empts the two questions in every consultant's head: *"what about the other
things I do besides BRSR?"* (the engine + modules cover them) and *"if I pay, I'll want
more"* (yes — the compounding roadmap **is** the value).

### How to frame it in the demo (≈90 seconds, after the free-tool demo)
The free tool is the hero (~80% of your time). Then one transition line: **"When you
move from preparing to actually collecting the data, that's where Pro comes in."** Show
Collect briefly — the requests dashboard, the auto-reminders, emissions auto-computed
and attributed, the draft. Frame the modules + knowledge centers as **roadmap**, and be
honest about what's built vs coming (never demo vapourware).

### If someone asks "how much / is it monthly?"
An honest deflect that doubles as research:
> "Pro's in early access — I'm setting the pricing *with* input from consultants like
> you. It'll match how you actually work, probably **per engagement** rather than a
> year-round fee, since BRSR is seasonal. What would feel fair to you?"

That does three things at once: shows you've already thought about the seasonality
they're worried about, avoids committing, and turns the question into a conversation
(and a lead).

### Your private cheat-note (do NOT say the number on the call)
Internally, the current lean is **per-client / per-engagement pricing** — it matches how
consultants bill their own clients and sidesteps the "why pay all year for a 6-month
tool" objection. A small annual **"workspace" tier** (knowledge centers + all modules)
comes later, once there's enough year-round value to justify it. This is context so you
can field questions confidently, *not* a price to quote. (Full recommendation lands
after the WhatsApp analysis.)

## 5. How it works under the hood (the mental model you must own)

You don't need to know every line of code. You need a clear, confident mental model
so that when someone asks "how does it actually do X," you can answer in plain
English. Here it is.

### The big picture: two halves
Saaksh is **two products in one**:
- **The free readiness tool** — runs **entirely in the user's web browser**. No
  account, no server doing the work, nothing stored anywhere but the user's own
  device.
- **Collect (the paid tier)** — has a **secure backend** (a database + email) because
  its whole job is to collect and track data from other people. This part
  deliberately stores data, securely.

When you say "client data never leaves your browser," that's about the **free tool**
and it's **literally true** — there is no server call when it generates a report.
Collect is the part that stores data, and it's access-controlled.

### How the free tool actually generates a report (no magic, no live AI)
Think of it like a very well-organised **rules engine with a built-in rulebook**:
1. The **rulebook** is a set of data files bundled inside the app — the full BRSR
   format (all 108 disclosure fields across 9 principles), pre-extracted from the
   **official SEBI BRSR Format and the ICAI Background Material (2024 edition)**.
   Every field carries its **SEBI/ICAI page citation**.
2. When the consultant fills the intake form (industry, size, which compliance
   filings the client already does, etc.), the app runs **logic in the browser**
   that compares each BRSR field against what those existing filings already cover.
3. That produces the **status of each field**: *already tracked* (the data exists in
   a filing the client already submits), *needs verification* (partially covered), or
   *collect fresh* (not covered anywhere). For service-sector clients it also marks
   manufacturing-only disclosures (air emissions, effluent, EIA, product reclaim) as
   *not applicable*.
4. The report you see is just that logic's output, rendered nicely. It's
   **regenerated from the saved intake form** each time, which is why a refresh
   rebuilds it instantly.

No server, no database, no AI is involved in producing the gap analysis. It's
deterministic: same inputs → same output, every time. That's a feature — it means
it's auditable and it can be free.

### What's in the "rulebook" (this is the real depth)
- **108 BRSR Section-C fields** (68 essential + 40 leadership) across 9 principles,
  plus the Section A/B general disclosures — each cited to SEBI/ICAI.
- **Compliance-filing overlaps** — a mapping of "if the client already files PCB
  consent / PAT / Hazardous Waste / EPR etc., here are the BRSR fields that data
  already covers." This is what makes the gap analysis smart instead of generic.
- **Cross-framework mappings** — every relevant BRSR field linked to its equivalent
  in **GRI, TCFD, IFRS S1/S2, and TNFD**, plus a crosswalk to **MSCI and S&P
  Global/DJSI** rating criteria.
- **Per-principle best practices** (India + international standards: SBTi, ISO,
  GHG Protocol, ZLD, etc.).
- **Plain-English explanations** on all 108 fields ("what it's asking").

That curated, cited content is the moat — far more than the UI.

### The calculators
GHG (Scope 1 & 2), energy, and water calculators are built into the relevant fields.
The consultant types activity data (litres of diesel, kWh of grid power, etc.) and it
computes the emissions using **published, cited factors** — the CEA grid emission
factor and IPCC fuel factors. Pure formulas, in the browser, with the factor and its
source shown on every line. **No number is invented** — it's either entered by a human
or computed by a cited formula.

### Where AI is used (and where it deliberately isn't)
Be precise here, because people will ask. **AI is used in exactly two narrow places,
and never to compute or invent numbers:**
1. **Free tool — the per-field explanations.** The "what it's asking" text on each of
   the 108 fields was **written by an AI once, offline, by me running a script** — and
   then shipped as **static text** baked into the app. So when a consultant uses the
   tool, there is **no live AI call and nothing about their client is sent anywhere**.
   It's pre-written, like a glossary.
2. **Paid Collect tier — an optional narrative draft.** After data is collected, the
   consultant can ask it to turn the numbers into a short paragraph per principle. The
   AI is **grounded**: it's given only the collected facts and told never to invent or
   change a number (it writes "[to be completed]" for gaps). The actual figures still
   come from the deterministic calculation engine, not the AI.

The one-liner: **"AI explains and drafts. It never computes or invents. Every number
is either entered by a human or computed by a cited formula."** (The AI runs on Groq's
open-source models, which keeps it cheap — but you don't need to volunteer that.)

### Collect (the paid backend), briefly
Its job: collecting BRSR data from a client's scattered teams without manual chasing.
The consultant creates a collection, adds the data owners, and the system sends each a
**branded request email**; owners fill a **no-login form** (and attach evidence like
bills/invoices); the consultant gets a **submission alert**; **auto-reminders** go out
on a schedule; emissions are **auto-computed with attribution** (which number came from
whom, via which cited factor); and it generates a **draft** of the responses. Under the
hood that's a database (Supabase), transactional email (Resend), and a daily scheduled
job (Vercel Cron), all access-controlled. You don't need more detail than that.

### The stack (only if a developer asks)
Next.js (React, TypeScript) on Vercel. The free tool is client-side, rendered from
bundled JSON. Collect adds a Supabase Postgres backend (row-level-security locked),
Resend for email, and a Vercel cron job. That's it.

## 6. Objection handling — "this isn't special, we could build it ourselves"

This is the one to nail. **Don't get defensive — agree, then reframe.** Confidence
here comes from honesty, not from pretending it's rocket science.

**The disarming open:** "Honestly? You could build the gap-analysis checklist
yourself — and that's exactly why I made it free. The checklist isn't the hard part.
The hard parts are the ones you'd have to keep doing forever."

Then the five real reasons it's worth using rather than building:

1. **Maintained, cited accuracy — the recurring cost.** BRSR has changed repeatedly
   (the format, BRSR Core, the March 2025 amendments), and the emission factors
   (CEA, IPCC) update too. Mapping the 108 fields once is a weekend. Keeping it
   correct and **cited** every time SEBI, ICAI or CEA move is an ongoing job. With
   Saaksh, that maintenance is my problem, not yours — your checklist never quietly
   goes out of date.

2. **The depth is already in there.** SEBI page citations on every field, the
   compliance-filing overlaps, cross-framework mapping to GRI/TCFD/IFRS/TNFD, the
   MSCI/DJSI crosswalk, per-principle best practices, plain-English explanations on
   all 108 fields. That's months of curated domain content — not something you'd
   rebuild on a Sunday.

3. **The collection engine is real software.** Chasing data from a client's scattered
   teams is the #1 time-sink in every engagement (your own words, Priya). Collect is
   branded emails + auto-reminders + no-login submission + evidence capture + emissions
   attribution + a draft. That's a backend with email infrastructure — months of work
   to build and maintain, not a spreadsheet.

4. **Knowledge centers are coming — and nobody has them.** The real pain isn't the
   checklist; it's that people don't know *how to answer* the questions. Per-principle
   "what an organisation should actually do," starting with **Principle 5 (Human
   Rights)** where Indian expertise is thin. That's expertise + content, the genuine
   differentiator.

5. **Your time is the expensive thing.** A consultant's billable hour is worth far
   more than a subscription. Time spent building and babysitting internal tooling is
   time not spent on clients — and home-built tools rot the moment you stop
   maintaining them. Plus a polished, cited, branded report in front of *your* client
   builds *your* credibility.

**The judo close:** "If the free checklist is all you need, take it — no login, and
I'll keep it accurate so you don't have to. The paid pieces are the ones that are
genuinely painful to build and maintain. I'd rather you spend your time on clients
than on tooling."

**Mindset:** your goal at the webinar isn't to "win" against a skeptic. It's to be
calm, honest, and let the live demo do the persuading. If one person is determined to
build their own, fine — most won't, and the free tool still gets them in the door.

## 7. Technical Q&A cheat-sheet

Short, confident answers. If you don't know one, see §8 — "I'll follow up" is a
strong answer.

- **"Where is my client's data stored? Is it safe?"**
  Free tool: nowhere outside your own browser — it never gets uploaded, there's no
  server involved in making the report. Collect (the paid tier) stores data in a
  secure, access-controlled database, visible only to you.

- **"How accurate is it? Where does the content come from?"**
  The official SEBI BRSR Format and the ICAI Background Material (2024 edition), with
  the page citation shown on every field. Emission factors come from CEA and IPCC,
  with the source noted on each calculation.

- **"Does it use AI? Will it make up numbers?"**
  AI is used in exactly two places and never to compute or invent numbers: the field
  explanations (pre-written once, shipped as static text — no live AI when you use
  it), and an optional grounded narrative draft in the paid tier that's told never to
  invent or change a figure. Every number is entered by a human or computed by a cited
  formula.

- **"Is it up to date with the latest SEBI amendments?"**
  Yes — it includes the March 2025 amendments. Keeping it current as SEBI updates is
  the ongoing work behind the tool.

- **"Does it handle service companies, not just manufacturers?"**
  Yes. You set the business type, and it marks manufacturing-only disclosures (air
  emissions, effluent discharge, EIAs, product reclaim) as "not applicable" for
  service-sector clients, so the gap analysis isn't cluttered with irrelevant fields.

- **"What about Scope 3 / CBAM / other frameworks?"**
  Scope 1 & 2 are built in now; Scope 3 and a CBAM module are on the roadmap.
  Cross-framework mapping to GRI, TCFD, IFRS and TNFD is already there.

- **"Is it really free? What's the catch?"**
  The readiness tool is genuinely free, no login. The paid tier is Collect — the
  engine for collecting data from the client's team — plus advanced modules and (soon)
  the knowledge centers.

- **"Can I manage multiple clients in it?"**
  Right now it holds one client's report at a time on a given device — a multi-client
  workspace is a near-term improvement on the roadmap. (Be honest about this; don't
  pretend otherwise.) You can keep separate exports per client today, and back up /
  restore a client's work as a file.

- **"What if I lose my work — clear my browser, switch laptops?"**
  Your work auto-saves on the device, so a refresh or restart is safe. If you clear
  your browser data or switch browsers it starts fresh — so there's a "Back up work"
  button that saves a file you can restore on another machine, plus PDF/CSV exports.

- **"Can I export it? Can it be white-labelled / branded?"**
  Yes — a CSV of every data point and a clean, client-ready PDF brief, both generated
  on your device. (Deeper white-labelling is a possible future paid option.)

- **"Who built it / is there a team?"**
  Be honest and own it: "I built it — I work in climate compliance and this is the
  tool I wished I had. It's early, and feedback from people like you is exactly what
  shapes it." Solo-founder honesty is a strength with this audience, not a weakness.

- **(Developer in the room) "What's the stack?"**
  Next.js / React / TypeScript on Vercel; the free tool is client-side from bundled
  JSON; Collect adds a Supabase Postgres backend (row-level-security locked), Resend
  email, and a scheduled cron job.

## 8. Honesty guardrails — don't over-claim

Your credibility with this audience is everything. A few rules:

- **Don't quote a specific number you can't defend.** If asked "what CEA factor do you
  use," say "the current published CEA grid factor, shown on the calculation with its
  source" — don't blurt a version number you're unsure of. Precise-but-wrong is worse
  than general-but-right.
- **Be upfront about current limits:** one client at a time per device (multi-client is
  coming); onboarding to the paid tier is currently manual ("talk to me and I'll set
  you up"); Collect's emails go live once the sending domain is verified. Naming limits
  honestly builds more trust than hiding them.
- **The domain is still `brsr-consultant-kit.vercel.app`** while a Saaksh domain is
  sorted — if someone notices, "the product's now called Saaksh; the address catches
  up shortly" is a fine answer.
- **"I don't know — let me check and get back to you" is a strong answer, not a weak
  one.** Better still: "Great question, I'd rather be accurate than guess — can I grab
  your email and follow up?" Now a hard question became a lead.
