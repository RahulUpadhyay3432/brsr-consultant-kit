# Design Brief — BRSR Consultant Kit

> Read this before forming any opinion about how the product should look. It describes
> who uses this tool, what they are trying to do, and how the product should make them
> feel. It deliberately says nothing about colour, type, or layout — those are yours to
> decide. Understand the person first; the design follows from that.

---

## What the product is

A working tool for independent ESG consultants in India who prepare **BRSR** (Business
Responsibility and Sustainability Reporting) filings for companies. BRSR is a sustainability
disclosure mandated by India's market regulator (SEBI) for listed companies: ~108 data points
across 9 principles covering emissions, water, energy, workforce, human rights, governance,
and community impact. It is detailed, regulated, and consequential — a filing that is wrong or
unsupported is a real professional and legal risk.

The product has two halves, one product:

1. **Readiness workspace.** The consultant enters a few facts about their client and instantly
   sees a gap analysis of all 108 disclosure fields: what is already covered by the client's
   existing compliance filings, what needs verifying, and what must be collected fresh. Plus a
   suggested materiality shortlist for the client's industry and a crosswalk showing how BRSR
   maps to international frameworks (GRI, TCFD, IFRS, MSCI). This half runs entirely in the
   browser — no login, nothing stored.

2. **Collect.** The consultant runs a data-collection campaign for a client: assigns specific
   fields to the right people inside the client's organisation (the energy manager for
   electricity and fuel, HR for headcount, EHS for water), sends each a branded request, tracks
   who has responded, auto-computes the client's GHG emissions from the submitted values with
   full traceability, and generates a printable draft of the filing.

## Who uses it

One person. An independent ESG consultant, or a tiny practice. In India.

- **Competent, not technical.** They understand sustainability, regulation, and their clients'
  businesses deeply. They are not engineers and have no patience for tools that need a manual.
- **Time-poor and accountable.** They run several client engagements at once, each lasting
  months. They are personally responsible for the accuracy of every number they file. A mistake
  is their reputation.
- **Working against friction.** Their single biggest time-sink is chasing data: the numbers live
  with different people inside the client company, and those people are slow to respond. Much of
  the job is gentle, repeated chasing.
- **Proud professionals.** They are building a practice. How their work *looks* is part of how
  they are judged. They open this tool in front of their own clients — CFOs, sustainability
  heads, plant EHS managers — often on a screen in a meeting.

## The job they are hiring this product to do

> "When I take on a BRSR client, help me quickly see what I need to collect, what they already
> have, and how to deliver it credibly — so I look expert and save days of manual work."

Three layers, and the product should serve all three:

- **Functional:** see the gaps, collect the data, compute the numbers, produce the filing.
- **Emotional:** feel **in control and competent** instead of overwhelmed and anxious. The work
  is genuinely stressful — regulatory weight, unresponsive people, deadlines, accountability.
  The product's job is to absorb that stress, not add to it.
- **Social:** look like a serious, well-run practice. Every screen should be something they are
  proud to show a client without editing or apologising.

## How the product should feel

This is the part to internalise. Not rules — a target.

- **Calm authority.** Like a well-run professional practice. Everything has a place; nothing
  shouts; the user feels looked after. Confidence through restraint, not decoration.
- **The data is the hero.** The consultant's client information — statuses, numbers, progress,
  what's done and what's left — is the point. The interface should recede and let that
  information come forward. Legible at a glance, before reading.
- **Trustworthy.** This tool handles regulated disclosures and confidential client data, and it
  carries citations to official sources. It must *look* as accurate and credible as it is.
  Nothing flippant, nothing that feels like a toy or a generic template.
- **Effortless to read.** A consultant glances at a screen and immediately knows the one thing
  that matters: how ready am I, who hasn't responded, what's missing. The next action is always
  obvious.
- **Quiet delight, where earned.** A moment of craft when something completes, when data
  arrives, when a draft is generated. Not animation everywhere — a few small, intentional
  moments that make the tool feel alive and considered.
- **Built for repeat use.** This is opened weekly across a long engagement. It should feel
  better the more you know it. Density is acceptable when hierarchy is clear; this is a work
  tool, not a landing page.

## What it must not feel like

- Generic SaaS (interchangeable, templated, the same as a hundred other dashboards).
- A government or compliance portal (heavy, cold, bureaucratic, joyless).
- A spreadsheet dressed up as an app.
- Anything that tries to show everything at once and leaves the user not knowing where to look.
- Anything that feels cheap, playful, or unserious. This person's reputation rides on it.

---

## The stakes, in one line

A consultant should open this and feel: *this was made for me, by someone who understood my
job — and I trust it enough to put it in front of my client.*
